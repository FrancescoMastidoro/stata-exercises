## Background
The `census` dataset contains 1980 U.S. Census data for all 50 states, including population counts, regional classifications, and demographic breakdowns such as marriage and death rates. Being able to tabulate categorical variables and summarize distributions is essential in labor economics, where understanding group-level patterns — by region, sector, or demographic — is a routine first step before any formal analysis.

## Your tasks

1. **Load and inspect** — Load the dataset with `sysuse census` and run `describe`. How many observations does the dataset have, and what does one observation represent? Identify which variables are numeric and which, if any, are stored as strings.

2. **Summarize population** — Use `summarize` on the variable `pop` (total population). Note the mean, minimum, and maximum. Which single number tells you the most "typical" state population, and why might the mean be misleading here?

3. **Detailed distribution** — Re-run `summarize` on `pop` with the `detail` option. Compare the median (50th percentile) to the mean. Does the difference suggest that the distribution of state populations is skewed? In which direction?

4. **Tabulate a categorical variable and combine with summarize** — Use `tabulate` on the variable `region` to see how many states fall into each Census region. Then use `summarize pop` separately for states in region 1 and region 2 (use an `if` condition each time). Which of those two regions has the higher average state population?

5. *(Bonus)* **Weighted frequencies** — Run `tabulate region` again, but this time add the option to display the percentage of observations in each category. Then think about this: those percentages count *states*, not *people*. How would you use `summarize` to get a sense of which region contains the largest share of the total U.S. population? Implement your idea and interpret the result.
