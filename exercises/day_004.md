## Background
The `nlsw88` dataset contains 1988 survey data on working women from the National Longitudinal Survey, covering wages, hours, occupation, industry, and demographic characteristics. Being able to load a dataset, inspect its structure, and produce basic descriptive statistics is the absolute foundation of any empirical labor economics project — you cannot interpret a regression or test a hypothesis without first understanding what your variables actually look like.

## Your tasks

1. **Load and inspect** — Load the dataset with `sysuse nlsw88, clear` and run `describe`. How many observations and variables does the dataset contain? Identify at least two variables that are stored as labeled numeric (i.e., they display value labels rather than raw numbers) and note what real-world concept each one captures.

2. **Summarize a continuous variable** — Use `summarize` on the variable `hours` (usual hours worked per week). Record the mean, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum. Does the minimum value seem plausible for a survey of working women? Write a one-sentence interpretation as a comment in your do-file.

3. **Detailed distribution of hours** — Re-run `summarize` on `hours` with the `detail` option. Compare the 50th percentile (median) to the mean. Is the distribution of hours symmetric, or does it appear skewed? In which direction, and what might that suggest about the presence of part-time or very-long-hour workers in the sample?

4. **Tabulate a categorical variable, then compare groups** — Use `tabulate` on the variable `race` to see how many women fall into each racial category. Then use `summarize wage` with an appropriate `if` condition to compute average wages separately for the first and second racial categories shown in the tabulation. Which group has the higher mean wage, and how large is the gap relative to the overall mean wage?

5. *(Bonus)* **Occupation frequency and wage dispersion** — Use `tabulate` on `occupation` to identify the three most common occupations in the sample. Then, for each of those three occupations, use `summarize wage, detail` with an `if` condition to retrieve the median wage and the interquartile range (75th minus 25th percentile). Which occupation shows the greatest internal wage dispersion, and what might explain that pattern?

> **Fresh hint**: When a distribution has a small number of extremely large values at the top end, the mean gets pulled upward toward those outliers even if most observations are clustered much lower. The summary statistic that is *not* affected by extreme values at either end — because it only depends on the middle-ranked observation — is the one you should prefer here.
